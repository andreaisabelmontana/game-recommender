What it is

Recommendations from real review data

The dataset is the point. Amazon's video-game reviews are huge, sparse and skewed — a handful of blockbuster titles drown out a long tail of niche games, and most users have reviewed only one or two things. The build learns item-to-item relationships from this: games frequently loved by the same people are linked, so a title you rated highly pulls up its closest companions.

Item-based collaborative filtering is the right tool here precisely because it's robust to the mess: relationships between games are far more stable than the fickle, sparse profiles of individual users. It's a lesson in choosing the method that fits the data you actually have.

Architecture

How a recommendation is built

From raw reviews to a related-games list, the pipeline is steady:

  1. Ingest

    Load and clean the review data into users, items and ratings.

  2. Sparsify

    Build the sparse interaction matrix the algorithm operates on.

  3. Relate

    Compute item–item similarities across the whole catalogue.

  4. Recommend

    For a seed game, retrieve and rank its nearest companions.

  5. Validate

    Measure hit-rate on held-out interactions and correct for popularity bias.

Reflection

What rebuilding it taught me

  • The data picks the method. Sparse, skewed reviews make item-based CF the natural fit — stable item relationships beat fragile user profiles.
  • Implicit signals need confidence. A review isn't a clean rating; weighting how much to trust each interaction matters more than the star value.
  • Popularity bias is sneaky. Without correction, the recommender just keeps suggesting the blockbusters everyone already knows.
  • Sparsity is an engineering problem. Half the work is representing millions of interactions so the maths even runs.
  • Offline metrics are a proxy. Hit-rate is useful, but it only hints at whether a human would actually click play.
